Unique Adaptations

Mangroves exhibit remarkable physiological adaptations including salt exclusion, ultrafiltration at root membranes, and specialized root structures for gas exchange in anoxic sediments.

  • Pneumatophores (aerial roots)
  • Viviparous germination
  • Salt-secreting leaves
  • Ultrafiltration roots

Carbon Sequestration

Mangroves are among the most carbon-rich forests in the tropics, storing an average of 1,023 Mg carbon per hectare in their biomass and soils (Donato et al., 2011).

Ecological Importance

  • Coastal Protection

    Mangroves reduce wave energy by 66-99% and can prevent $65 billion annually in flood damages (Men√©ndez et al., 2020). Their complex root systems stabilize shorelines and reduce erosion.

  • Nursery Habitats

    75% of commercially caught fish spend part of their life cycles in mangroves. A single hectare can yield 767 kg of fish annually (UNEP, 2014).

  • Water Purification

    Mangroves filter terrestrial runoff, removing 90-100% of nitrate and phosphate loads. They process heavy metals through sedimentation and microbial activity.

  • Biodiversity Hotspots

    Support 4,011 faunal species including 327 threatened species on the IUCN Red List (Polidoro et al., 2010).
